Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

How does understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefit people?

Neuroplasticity defines the process in which the brain goes through structural and functional changes in response to stimuli and is not static. The brain can alter its connections through forming new ones and pruning others depending on its response to learning.

Understanding the principles of neuroplasticity is beneficial as it can change the mindset that our brains are unchanging. Instead it opens up the idea that through use, repetition, and time, our brains can develop and create stronger neural pathways as we continue to learn, meaning that we are not restrained by the idea that our brain has a fixed capacity, instead it means that our brain can change and restructure so that we can continue to reach greater heights .


How might you engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit?

I’ll engage with the principles of neuroplasticity through understanding that the way I think and learn can affect the way my brain changes and creates neural connections. By changing my thinking towards a more “I can do this” attitude, I am increasing my ability to learn more.


What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

I’ll increase my neuroplasticity through using it and through repetition, such as learning a new concept and attempting to try to use it and understand it and then repeat this over several days to ensure that I am not just learning and forgetting it. This way, i can build up the neural connections in my brain for the new concept that i am learning.

What is Growth Mindset and why it is relevant?

A mindset is the way a person views learning, their abilities, or skill when faced with challenges, hiccups and setbacks.

A person with a growth mindset believes that they can overcome their struggles and view challenges as a way they can improve their knowledge and skills. They believe that over time through learning, effort and practise an individual is able to learn new things that they are not yet capable of and be able to achieve it. This means that their abilities are always changing and not fixed or static.

People with a fixed mindset are restricting themselves in the sense that they do not think they can improve current skills and knowledge or learn new skills or get better at something because their skills and traits are unchangeable. Because the mindset is already set, it can lead to easily giving up in the face of challenges and putting in less effort into improving oneself as they don’t believe that it will work.

In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?

Personally, I have learnt about the growth mindset and that our brains are neuroplastic, however, after listening to Carol Dweck talk about responding to student results with “yet” and “not yet” in terms of reaching a goal or achieving a result, I was surprised at how much of a difference the words can affect a students mindset and change it from a fixed one to a growth mindset where they can view the challenge from a black or white, pass or fail, to, you haven’t reached yet. The “yet” recognises the progress you have made but also tells you that there is still more you can achieve.

This blog goes into more detail about having a growth mindset and comparing it to a fixed mindset, there is also a link to a TEDTalk where Carol Dweck talks about this topic and I found it an interesting read.

Reflect and discuss how these understandings may shape or influence your learning plan and strategy.

Understanding that the way I view a challenge can change the way I learn and engage with the content has made me more aware that I should view challenges as my opportunity to learn a new concept or how to implement a new skill and not view it as just a pass or fail. Even if I fail to get the correct answer, I am learning what is working and what is not working, and that I will eventually get to the answer. This way, I won't be inclined to give up because I don't get it, but to pursue it because I just don’t get it… Yet.
